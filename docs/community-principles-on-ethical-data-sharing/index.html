Data Scientist Code of Ethics

This code of ethics for data sharing is created and proposed for adoption by the data science community to reflect the behaviors and principles for the responsible and ethical use and sharing of data by data scientists.

As a community-driven crowdsourced effort, you can join the the discussion and contribute to the next version of the Community Principles on Ethical Data Sharing.

Overview

The Community Principles on Ethical Data Sharing (CPEDS) are actively being worked on by a powerful community of data scientists and data organizations focused on defining responsible behaviors for sharing and using data to positively impact the lives of individuals. The goal of this initiative is to develop the first-ever community-driven code of ethics for data sharing and to give data scientists a standard set of easily digestible, recognizable principles for guiding their behaviors.

It is not intended to be all encompassing. Rather, these principles will provide academia and industry, for specific sectors, and individual data scientists with a common set of foundational guidelines for driving the development of standards, curriculums, and best practices for the ethical use and sharing of data, ultimately advancing the responsible use of data as a collective force for good.

As a living document, the principles will be reviewed regularly and D4GX will serve as a convener for voting and updating the Community Principles on Ethical Data Sharing. For the first time, the data science community will come together virtually and in-person to vote on the initial set of principles, collaboratively turning the results of the working groups into a code of ethics focused on the issues of data sharing.

Working Groups

This Code of Ethics has been created, and will be maintained, by the data science community. The principles aim to guide the responsible use of data by anyone who shares and uses data in ways that impact the lives of individuals. The Code is not intended to be complete, but instead to provide core guidelines for responsible data sharing and use by academics, companies, governments, and independent researchers.

Thought Diversity

Thought Diversity is an important tool to combat individual dogma and groupthink while helping to foster a culture of inclusion, diversity, humility and tolerance. A culture such as this provides space that fosters creative and innovative thinking by enabling problem-solving to flourish. It is our responsibility foster a diverse and inclusive culture in the data science community by putting in the work necessary to make our communities more accessible to people from diverse backgrounds and marginalized groups.

Bias

Every human dataset has the potential to replicate or exacerbate social bias on a large scale, especially when that data is used to support algorithmic decision making. This occurs when data recorded in unjust conditions is collected, manipulated, modeled, and/or visualized without adequate bias mitigation techniques. Bias is a concern as outputs have the potential to be unfair, misunderstood, and/or damaging for large numbers of people.

Responsible Communication

Responsible Communications focuses on the direct relationship between the Data Scientist and the public. In every situation, there is a balance point between the public and the data scientist. Ethical considerations should drive where that balance point exists and support a two-way, open flow of written, visual and spoken information between the data science and the public. This should be broader than a specific industry or discipline. Specifically, responsible communications includes the following themes: accountability to the public, deciphering black-box algorithms, accessibility to lay audiences, and education of the public.

Provenance and Ownership

Clear data provenance promotes trust, aids transparency, and reduces barriers to sharing data. Collecting, propagating, storing, and curating provenance incurs costs, but these costs improve the value of the data and the decisions that the data inform. Maintaining data provenance is a key responsibility, and it should be done in a way that is mindful of the privacy trade-offs.

Endeavor to provide transparency, in order to build trust, by design, and as default practice, and to do so responsibly, with context, and while preserving others rights to an explanation, recourse, and rectification.
