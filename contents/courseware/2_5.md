		<h2>The Shaffer 4 C's of Data Visualization</h2>
	</div>
	<div class="half">
		<ol>
			<li><b>Clear - easily seen; sharply defined</b>
			<ul>
				<li>Who is the audience</li>
				<li>What is the message</li>
				<li>Clarity is more important than aesthetics</li>
			</ul></li>
			<li><b>Clean - thorough; complete; unadulterated</b>
			<ul>
				<li>Avoid over-labeling axis and data points</li>
				<li>Avoid too many or too prominent grid lines</li>
				<li>Use proper number formatting</li>
				<li>Use the correct chart type for the message</li>
				<li>Use optimal color choice</li>
			</ul></li>
			<li><b>Concise - brief but comprehensive</b>
			<ul>
				<li>Not minimalist but not verbose</li>
			</ul></li>
			<li><b>Captivating - attract and hold by beauty or excellence</b>
			<ul>
				<li>Does it capture attention?</li>
				<li>Is it interesting?</li>
				<li>Does it tell a story?</li>
			</ul></li>
		</ol>

	<div class="full">
		<h2>Edward Tufte's Rules for Data Visualization</h2>
	</div>
	<div class="half">
		<ol>
			<li><b>Show Your Data</b> - Once data is gathered, the first step is to show it or determine how to show it based on the type of data, your audience, and your medium.</li>
			<li><b>Use Graphics</b> - When you can’t communicate your message effectively through words, use pictures and graphics.</li>
			<li><b>Avoid Chartjunk</b> - Chartjunk is a term coined by Tufte that advocates for the removal of unnecessary or confusing visual elements from charts and graphs.</li>
			<li><b>Maximize Data-Ink Ratio</b> - Compare the ink on the page associated with the data vs. all of the ink on the page and erase redundant data-ink.</li>
			<li><b>Use Labels</b> - Labels are informative guides and are essential in data design. Effective labels focus on clarity, readability, and differentiation. Labels should stand out from the data.</li>
			<li><b>Utilize Micro/Macro</b> - The clarity of the macro is determined by the quality and quantity of the micro.</li>
			<li><b>Separate Layers</b> - Create layers by adjust the tone, color, and/or weight of visual elements.</li>
			<li><b>Multiples</b> - Use consistency in design to highlight differences or inconsistencies.</li>
			<li><b>Color</b> - Choose colors wisely. Use color to create emphasis, draw attention. Poor color choice can lead to confusion and disruption.</li>
			<li><b>Understand Narrative</b> - A proper narrative focuses on time and space to tell it’s story.</li>
		</ol>
		<h6>Obtained from <i><a target="_blank" href="https://www.amazon.com/Visual-Display-Quantitative-Information/dp/1930824130">The Visual Display of Quantitative Information</a></i> by Edward Tufte</h6>

	<div class="full">
		<h2>Gestalt Principles of Perception</h2>
	</div>
	<div class="half">
		<ol>
			<li><b>Similarity Principle</b> - Elements that share similar attributes, color, or shape, are perceived as a whole.</li>
			<li><b>Proximity Principle</b> - Elements tend to be perceived as aggregated into groups if they are near or connected.</li>
			<li><b>Common Fate Principle</b> - Elements that move together are perceived as a whole.</li>
			<li><b>Enclosure Principle</b> - Objects that appear to have a boundary or a continuation around them are perceived as a whole.</li>
			<li><b>Continuity Principle</b> - Elements, unites, or groups aligned with each other are perceived as a whole.</li>
			<li><b>Closure Principle</b> - Open structures can be easily be perceived as closed/complete.</li>
		</ol>
	</div>

<section>
	<div class="full">
		<h2>Color Theory and Application</h2>
	</div>
	<div class="half">
		<h4>Color can be very subjective</h4>
		<ul>
			<li>Use of color in visualization can be essential for delivering a message , making a point, capturing attention, influencing feelings, and branding an image.</li>
			<li>Reactions to individual colors can vary between people and culture. However, color theory is an advanced and evidence-based science that can help us make appropriate decisions about color choice in a visualization.</li>
			<li>Here are some general color choice guidelines for visualizations:
			<ul>
				<li>Use similar colors for similar variables</li>
				<li>Use legends to explain colors</li>
				<li>Consider mixing greyscale with colors to create accents and highlights</li>
				<li>Use high contrast ratios for legibility</li>
				<li>Test how colors appear next to each other and adjust accordingly</li>
				<li>Use intuitive colors and consider culture (reds vs. green, light vs. dark, converging vs. diverging, color-blind palettes.)</li>
			</ul></li>
		</ul>
	</div>

<section>
	<div class="full">
		<h2>The Don'ts of Data Visualization</h2>
		<h4>You can avoid many of the DONT’S by following the best practices you’ve seen already. There are a few additional points worth calling out:</h4>
		<ul>
			<li>When attempting to create a coherent set of visuals, avoid fragmentation or requiring viewers to scroll or move from one page to another in order to obtain a full picture of a visualization.</li>
			<li>Keep it clean but not so minimal that viewers get confused by lack of context.
				<ul><li>The opposite is also true, avoid excessive detail *See Tufte’s Data-Ink Ratio</li></ul></li>
			<li>Avoid Useless variety. Don’t create a variety of visualizations to tell a story just because you want to. Display only what is necessary and don’t force viewers to work hard to understand what they are seeing.</li>
			<li>Be cautious of misleading or misrepresentation of data. Don’t mislead viewers by using minimal context, inappropriate scales, etc.</li>
			<li>Avoid clutter or Chartjunk. Remove distractions and decorations. Arrange visuals in a logical flow. Make more important data stand out and get noticed immediately. </li>
			<li>Do not misuse or overuse color.</li>
		</ul>
	</div>
