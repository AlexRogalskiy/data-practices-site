<section>
    <div class="full text-align-left">
        <h2 class="larger">What is a query?</h2>
        <p>Just like it sounds, it is a question that you are asking your data source that returns (if you asked nicely enough) the data that you want, without all the rest of the clutter.</p>    
        <p>If you are a beginner, we'll be starting with SQL. This Structured Query Language is the standard language for relational databases. So naturally there are a ton of variants and subtle differences to trip you up.</p>
        <p>If SQL is old hat for you, you can try your hand at SPARQL as we go. This query language is for knowledge graphs and the semantic web. Welcome to the future! (wooshing sound)</p>
    </div>
    <aside class="notes">
    Notes
    </aside>
</section>

<section>
    <div class="full">
        <h2 class="larger">The Anatomy of a Query</h2>
    </div>
    <div class="half text-align-left">
        <h4>SQL</h4>
        <p>Dear Database, please give me all the <b>names</b> of people in my <b>address book</b>.</p>
        <pre>SELECT <b>names</b> FROM <b>address_book;</b></pre>
        <p>In alphabetical order?</p>
        <pre>SELECT <b>names</b> FROM <b>address_book</b> <u>ORDER BY</u> names ASCENDING;</pre>
        <p>How many are there?</p>
        <pre>SELECT COUNT(<b>names</b>) FROM <b>address_book</b>;</pre>
    </div>
    <div class="half text-align-left">
        <h4>SPARQL</h4>
        <p>Dear Semantic Web, please give me all the <b>names</b> of people in my <b>address_book</b>.</p>
        <pre>SELECT ?names FROM ?address_book</pre>
        <p>In alphabetical order?</p>
        <pre>SELECT ?names FROM ?address_book ORDER BY ASC(?names)</pre>
        <p>How many are there?</p>
        <pre>SELECT (count(distinct ?names) as ?count) 
        FROM ?address_book</pre>
    </div>

</section>

<section>
    <div class="full">
        <h2 class="larger">What is Sampling</h2>
        <p>A broad term applied to statistical analysis techniques used to capture a representative <b>subset</b> (“sample”) of data points to identify patterns and allow study of the larger dataset (“population”) without the overhead of oppressive amounts of data.</p>
    </div>
</section>

<section>
    <div class="full">
        <h2>Types of Sampling</h2>
    </div>
    <div class="one-fifth">
        <h4>Random</h4>
        <p>"Drawing Names from a Hat"</p>
    </div>
    <div class="one-fifth">
        <h4>Systemic</h4>
        <p>"Count Off"</p>
        <p>(list 1,2,3,4,1,2,3,4)</p>
        <p>Select "all the 4s"</p>
    </div>
    <div class="one-fifth">
        <h4>Convenience</h4>
        <p>"Take the first data I encounter"</p>
        <p>Easiest and Worst</p>
    </div>
    <div class="one-fifth">
        <h4>Cluster</h4>
        <p>"Group populations together" (often by geo)</p>
        <p>Select some of these complete groups</p>
    </div>
    <div class="one-fifth">
        <h4>Stratified</h4>
        <p>"Combining methods"</p>
        <p>Divides population into groups ("strata") by some characteristic (ex: sec, race, etc)</p>
        <p>A sample is taken from each group using Random, Systemic, or Convenience</p>
    </div>
</section>

    <div class="full">
        <h2 class="larger">What is Exploratory Viz?</h2>
        <p><b>Exploratory visualization</b> is when a user has no set goal or outcome in mind. You use this technique when you don’t know what is in the data and are seeking to understand the data better and uncover patterns in the overall data.</p>
        <p>This is contrasted with <b>explanatory visualization</b> where you already know what is in the data and are seeking to tell that story to a specific audience. While the exploratory viz is done during this exploration phase, explanatory viz is done during the reporting phase and should be designed to highlight the specific story that you wish to tell.</p>
    </div>

<section>
    <div class="full">
        <h2 class="larger">Feature Engineering</h2>
        <p>"Feature engineering is the science (and art) of extracting more information from existing data. You are not adding any new data here, but you are actually making the data you already have more useful.</p>
        <p>For example, let's say you are trying to predict foot fall in a shopping mall based on dates. If you try and use the dates directly, you may not be able to extract meaningful insights from the data. This is because the foot fall is less affected by the day of the month than it is by the day of the week. Now this information about day of week is implicit in your data. You need to bring it out to make your model better."</p>
        <p>(<a href="https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/blog/2016/01/guide-data-exploration/#four">https://www.analyticsvidhya.com/blog/2016/01/guide-data-exploration/#four</a>)</p>
